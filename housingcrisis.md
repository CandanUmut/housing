# The Global Housing Crisis: An Evidence-Based Investigation

**The world's wealthiest nations have made housing unaffordable for the majority of their working populations — and the data shows this was a policy choice, not an inevitability.** In the United States, the median home now costs five times the median household income, up from roughly 2.3 times in 1970. Half of all American renters spend more than 30% of their income on housing. A minimum-wage worker would need to work **116 hours per week** — nearly three full-time jobs — to afford a modest two-bedroom apartment anywhere in the country. Homelessness hit an all-time record of 771,480 people in 2024. These are not natural market outcomes. They reflect decades of policy decisions that transformed shelter into a speculative asset class, restricted supply through exclusionary zoning, and channeled hundreds of billions in annual mortgage interest from working families to financial institutions. This report compiles the hard data, traces the structural causes, documents the human toll, and evaluates what alternative models have actually worked — with honest caveats throughout.

## SECTION 1: THE DATA — HOW BAD IS IT REALLY?

### 1.1 The price-to-income ratio has nearly doubled since 1970

**Finding:** The traditional benchmark for housing affordability — a home costing roughly three times annual household income — held through approximately 1990 in the United States. It has since collapsed. The national ratio reached **5.0× in 2024**, with major coastal cities exceeding 10×. This pattern repeats across the Anglophone world, with Australia, Canada, and the UK all far above historic norms. Japan is the sole major economy where the ratio has *improved*.

The OECD price-to-income index uses different methodology than Demographia's absolute ratios, complicating cross-source comparisons.

### 1.2 Half of all American renters are now cost-burdened

**Finding:** The share of US renters spending more than 30% of income on rent has doubled since 1960, reaching a record **50%** in 2022. The divergence between rent growth and income growth is stark: since 1960, inflation-adjusted rents rose **75%** while real median renter income rose just **15%**. For minimum-wage earners, the situation is catastrophic — covering a modest two-bedroom apartment now requires nearly three full-time jobs at the federal minimum wage.

**Rent burden over time:**

| Year | % Cost-Burdened (>30%) | % Severely Burdened (>50%) |
|------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| 1960 | ~24% | <14% |
| 1970 | ~24–25% | <14% |
| 1980 | ~35% | ~18% |
| 1990 | ~36–38% | ~18–20% |
| 2000 | ~40% | ~20% |
| 2010 | ~49% | ~26% |
| 2022 | **50% (record)** | **27% (12.1M households)** |

The Harvard Joint Center for Housing Studies reports that the median renter household in 1960 spent less than a fifth of income on rent. By 2022, housing consumed **31%** of the median renter's income. The decade from 2000 to 2010 was the worst for renters: rents rose 12% in real terms while incomes fell 7%.

**Minimum wage and rent:**

In 1970, the federal minimum wage was $1.60/hour and median gross rent was $108/month. A full-time minimum-wage worker needed roughly **67.5 hours per month** (about 17 hours per week) to cover median rent — approximately two weeks' pay. In 2025, the federal minimum wage remains $7.25/hour (unchanged since 2009). The National Low Income Housing Coalition's annual "Out of Reach" report calculates a **Housing Wage** of **$33.63/hour** for a two-bedroom apartment at fair market rent — 4.6 times the federal minimum wage. A minimum-wage worker would need to work **116 hours per week** (2.9 full-time jobs) to afford that apartment at the 30%-of-income standard.

The NLIHC finding that **"in no state, metropolitan area, or county in the U.S. can a full-time worker earning the federal or prevailing minimum wage afford a modest two-bedroom rental home at fair market rent"** has held consistently across recent editions of the Out of Reach report and is verified as of 2025.

**Real wages vs. rent growth (indexed to 1970 ≈ 100):**

| Decade | Real Median Renter Income | Real Median Rent | Gap |
|--------|--------------------------|------------------|-----|
| 1970 | 100 (base) | 100 (base) | — |
| 1980 | ~97 | ~116 | Divergence begins |
| 1990 | ~100 | ~138 | Widening |
| 2000 | ~109 | ~145 | — |
| 2010 | ~102 | ~155 | Income fell in 2000s |
| 2022 | ~115 | ~175 | Record divergence |

**International context:** The US ranks among the worst in the OECD for renter affordability. The UK median renter spends roughly 32.5% of income on housing (2025), while Germany sits at a more manageable 22%. European bottom-quintile renters spend over 40% of disposable income on rent on average (IMF, 2021). Cross-country comparisons are complicated by differences in how housing costs are measured — gross vs. net of allowances, gross vs. disposable income.

Homeownership among adults under 35 has dropped from roughly 41% in 1980 to **36.3%** in 2024 — the lowest since 2019. The generational decline is sharper: **48% of Baby Boomers** owned homes by age 30, compared to just **33% of Millennials**. Early Gen Z data tracks even lower. This pattern of collapsing youth homeownership is replicated across the UK, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand.

In Canada, homeownership among 25–29-year-olds fell from 44.1% to 36.5% between 2011 and 2021. In Australia, only about one-third of 25–34-year-olds were homeowners by 2017. **Countries where homeownership has held steady** tend to share either post-communist mass privatization legacies (Eastern Europe: Romania, Croatia at ~90%), strong rental cultures with robust tenant protections (Germany, Switzerland), or sustained social housing investment (Finland, Austria).

### 1.4 Mortgage math reveals the true cost of homeownership

**Finding:** The word "mortgage" derives from Old French *mort gage* — literally **"death pledge."** The financial reality lives up to the etymology. At current interest rates (~7%), a buyer pays roughly **2.4 times the purchase price** of a home over a 30-year loan. The interest alone exceeds the original price. Monthly payments for the median US home have more than doubled since 2020 due to combined effects of price appreciation and rate increases.

**$300,000 home: 3% vs. 7% interest (30-year fixed):**

| Metric | At 3.0% | At 7.0% | Difference |
|--------|---------|---------|------------|
| Monthly payment (P&I) | **$1,265** | **$1,996** | +$731/mo (+58%) |
| Total paid over 30 years | **$455,332** | **$718,527** | +$263,195 |
| Total interest paid | **$155,332** | **$418,527** | +$263,195 |
| Interest as % of total | 34.1% | **58.2%** | — |
| Total paid as multiple of price | 1.52× | **2.40×** | — |

At 7%, the buyer pays **$418,527 in interest alone** — $118,527 more than the home's purchase price. The total cost of $718,527 confirms the claim that buyers pay the equivalent of approximately 2–3 homes over the loan's lifetime.

**Median US home: 2020 rates vs. 2024 rates:**

| Metric | $300K at ~3% (2020) | $407K at ~6.7% (2024) |
|--------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Monthly payment | ~$1,265 | ~$2,636 |
| Total paid over 30 years | ~$455,000 | ~$949,000 |
| Total interest paid | ~$155,000 | ~$542,000 |

A buyer in 2024 faces monthly payments **more than double** those of a 2020 buyer — driven by both price appreciation (+36%) and rate increases (from 3.11% to 6.72%). The 2020–2021 period represented the lowest mortgage rates in the Freddie Mac Primary Mortgage Market Survey's 50+ year history, with an annual average of 2.96% in 2021.

**Historical etymology:** Sir Edward Coke explained in his *Institutes of the Lawes of England* (1628) that the pledge becomes "dead" in two senses: it expires when the debt is fully repaid, or if the borrower defaults, their right to the property is "dead to him." The medieval Latin *mortuum wadium* contrasted with *vadium vivum* ("living pledge"), where rental income automatically reduced the debt — a structure closer to modern Islamic finance than to today's conventional mortgages.

### 1.5 Homelessness hit an all-time record while hidden housing insecurity affects millions

**Finding:** The 2024 HUD Point-in-Time count recorded **771,480 homeless people** — the highest figure ever recorded and an **18.1% increase** over 2023, the largest single-year jump in the survey's history. Family homelessness surged 39.4%. The PIT count captures only a fraction of housing insecurity: an estimated **3.7 million people** live "doubled up" with others due to economic hardship, and 1.4 million children were identified as homeless by schools. The sole bright spot is veteran homelessness, which has declined 55% since 2009 through targeted federal investment.

**PIT count trend:**

| Year | Total Homeless | Key Context |
|------|---------------|-------------|
| 2007 | 647,258 | Baseline year |
| 2010 | ~649,917 | Post-recession peak |
| 2016 | 549,928 | Historic low |
| 2020 | ~580,466 | Pandemic-era protections |
| 2023 | 653,104 | Prior record |
| 2024 | **771,480** | **All-time record (+18.1%)** |

Drivers of the 2024 surge include the expiration of COVID-era protections (Emergency Rental Assistance, eviction moratoriums, expanded child tax credit), rising rents, and housing supply shortages. Family homelessness doubled in 13 migration-affected communities.

**Hidden homelessness dwarfs the official count.** The PIT count is a single-night January snapshot that systematically undercounts hidden populations. A 2022 peer-reviewed study in *Housing Policy Debate* estimated **3.7 million people** living in doubled-up arrangements — roughly **5× the PIT count.** The Department of Education identified **1.4 million children** experiencing homelessness during the 2022–23 school year, of whom 84% were doubled up or in motels — categories excluded from HUD's PIT definition.

**Finland's Housing First model** demonstrates that reducing homelessness is a policy choice. Finland adopted Housing First as national policy in 2008, providing permanent housing immediately with no preconditions (sobriety, employment, treatment). Helsinki converted shelters into permanent apartments, going from 2,121 shelter beds in 1985 to just 52 by 2016. Long-term homelessness fell **~72% from 2008 to 2024**, reaching approximately 3,806 total homeless people (0.06% of population). A Tampere University study found this approach saves **€15,000–52,000 per person per year** compared to managing chronic homelessness through emergency services. Finland is one of only two EU countries where homelessness has consistently declined. However, in 2024–25 homelessness ticked upward for the first time in over a decade due to government austerity cuts — demonstrating the model's vulnerability to political shifts.

**Eviction data:** Princeton University's Eviction Lab estimates approximately **2.3 million eviction filings** and **900,000 actual evictions** annually in pre-pandemic years — roughly one filing for every 17 renter households. After pandemic moratoriums expired, filings rebounded sharply: **19 of 35 tracked cities** had higher filing rates in 2024 than pre-pandemic levels. Phoenix recorded a record-breaking 86,946 filings in 2024 — **one every six minutes.** Evictions disproportionately affect women (58% of defendants) and Black renters (36% of defendants vs. 28% of renter population). There is no comprehensive national eviction database; the GAO confirmed in 2024 that Eviction Lab estimates are the best available but likely undercount total evictions.

## SECTION 2: WHO BROKE THE SYSTEM AND HOW

### 2.1 Housing became a financial instrument in three stages

**Finding:** Housing's transformation from shelter to speculative asset occurred through three distinct phases: the creation of the secondary mortgage market (1968–1983), the deregulation era culminating in the 2008 crisis (1990s–2008), and post-crisis financialization 2.0 driven by quantitative easing and institutional investors (2009–present). Each phase further decoupled housing prices from their function as places to live.

**Phase 1 — Securitization (1968–1983):** In 1968, the Housing and Urban Development Act split Fannie Mae into a privatized entity and Ginnie Mae, which guaranteed the first mortgage pass-through security. Freddie Mac was created in 1970 to expand the secondary mortgage market. By 1983, Freddie Mac issued the first collateralized mortgage obligation (CMO). Scholars identify the 1970s as the decade when "the decoupling of the financial side of housing — mortgages and prices — from real economic activity" began across advanced economies (Socio-Economic Review, Oxford Academic).

**Phase 2 — Deregulation and the 2008 crisis:** The 1999 repeal of Glass-Steagall removed barriers between commercial and investment banking. By 2003, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac held over **$2 trillion** in mortgage holdings, up from 7% of the residential market in 1980. Private-label securitization exploded from 2003–2007, with unregulated conduits issuing MBS outside GSE oversight, feeding the subprime crisis. On September 7, 2008, both GSEs entered federal conservatorship, where they remain. Combined outstanding securities exceed **$7.7 trillion**.

**Phase 3 — QE and financialization 2.0:** Post-2008, the Federal Reserve's quantitative easing purchased nearly **90% of the increase in eligible mortgages from 2020 to 2022** (Brookings Institution). An IMF Working Paper (2023) found that while monetary easing decreases the mortgage payment burden, it "raises house prices, lowers housing affordability for first-time homebuyers, and increases housing wealth inequality." A Canadian study found QE produces nearly double the housing price increase of conventional monetary policy. Meanwhile, institutional investors began purchasing single-family homes at scale for the first time, marking "financialization 2.0."

**Real estate as share of household wealth:** As of Q4 2024, household real estate totaled **$48.1 trillion** — approximately 28.4% of total household net worth of $169.4 trillion (Federal Reserve Z.1 Financial Accounts). Globally, residential real estate comprises **$163 trillion**, representing roughly 75% of all global real estate value. The UN Special Rapporteur on Housing described the shift succinctly: "Housing is treated as a commodity — a vehicle for wealth and investment — rather than a social good."

### 2.2 Wall Street entered the landlord business — but the narrative needs nuance

**Finding:** Institutional investors entered the single-family rental market en masse after the 2008 crisis, purchasing foreclosed homes at discount prices. The six largest corporate landlords now own approximately **430,000 homes.** Their documented practices include higher eviction rates, aggressive rent increases, and maintenance failures compared to small landlords. However, they represent only **~3% of single-family rentals** nationally and **less than 0.5% of total single-family housing stock.** The widespread claim that "BlackRock is buying all homes" is factually wrong — it conflates BlackRock (an asset manager) with Blackstone (a private equity firm), and even Blackstone owns just 0.06% of US single-family homes. The real impact is intensely local.

**Major institutional SFR landlords (2025):**

| Company | Homes Owned | Background |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| Progress Residential (Pretium) | ~100,000 | PE-backed; largest SFR portfolio |
| Invitation Homes | 97,036 | Founded by Blackstone 2012; IPO 2017 |
| Blackstone (via HPA + Tricon) | ~62,000 | Re-entered via acquisitions 2021–2024 |
| American Homes 4 Rent | 60,337 | Founded 2012; IPO 2013 |
| Amherst Group | ~59,400 | Privately held |
| FirstKey Homes (Cerberus) | 52,000+ | Founded 2015 |

These six firms own roughly 430,000 of approximately **11.3 million** single-family rental properties. Mom-and-pop landlords (1–10 properties) own **82.6%** of all single-family rentals (John Burns Research & Consulting). However, **metro-level concentration is far higher**: institutional investors own **25% of the SFR market in Atlanta, 21% in Jacksonville, and 18% in Charlotte.**

A Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta study found corporate owners of 15+ properties were **8% more likely to file eviction notices** than small landlords. In Atlanta specifically, the overall SFR eviction filing rate was 7%, while institutional investors' rate was **20% — nearly three times higher.** The GAO (2024) found "mixed evidence" nationally but noted "institutional investment may increase rents and home prices, particularly in places with high rates of institutional ownership."

**The Airbnb effect is real but modest at city scale.** The most rigorous study (Garcia-López et al., *Journal of Urban Economics*, 2020) found that in Barcelona, Airbnb increased rents by **1.9%** and transaction prices by **4.6–5.3%** for the average neighborhood, with substantially larger effects in high-activity tourist areas. NYC's Comptroller estimated Airbnb was responsible for a **10% rental price increase from 2009 to 2016.** Effects are significant in specific neighborhoods but limited at the city-wide housing stock level.

**Vacant homes:** Approximately **15.1 million homes** sit vacant nationwide (10.3% of housing stock, the lowest rate in 25 years). However, the largest category is seasonal/recreational use. Investors held **882,300 vacant houses** as of Q3 2024, comprising **63% of all vacant residential properties** — a significant finding, though still a small fraction of total housing stock.

### 2.3 The interest system structurally front-loads wealth extraction

**Finding:** Mortgage amortization is designed so that in the early years — when most homeowners sell or refinance — the overwhelming majority of each payment goes to interest rather than principal. At current rates, **85% of the first monthly payment** is pure interest. The "tipping point" where more payment goes to principal than interest doesn't arrive until approximately **year 19** of a 30-year loan. Given that the average homeowner stays roughly 8 years, many never reach meaningful equity building. Michael Hudson estimates that 80% of bank loans in the US and UK are mortgages, generating approximately 70% of the economy's interest payments.

On a $350,000 loan at 6.375%, the first monthly payment of $2,184 splits into **$1,859 interest** (85.2%) and just **$324 principal** (14.8%). The total interest paid over 30 years: $318,861 — nearly equal to the original loan. At higher rates, the extraction is greater: a $400,000 loan at 6% generates approximately **$463,000 in total interest** — more than the home itself costs.

Annual mortgage interest flowing to the US banking sector is estimated at **$500–650 billion**, derived from approximately $13.2 trillion in household mortgage debt at a blended effective rate of 4–5%. This figure is substantial enough that Hudson characterizes the FIRE sector (Finance, Insurance, Real Estate) as parasitic on the productive economy: *"Instead of creating a mutually beneficial symbiosis with the economy of production and consumption, today's financial parasitism siphons off income needed to invest and grow."*

**Historical context on usury prohibition spans every major civilization.** The medieval Catholic Church explicitly banned usury, with the Third Lateran Council (1179) imposing excommunication and denial of Christian burial on usurers. Thomas Aquinas argued charging interest was "selling the same thing twice." The prohibition gradually weakened from the 16th century onward, with the Church formally acknowledging the legality of interest in 1828. The Ottoman Empire's waqf system funded housing, education, and healthcare through charitable endowments without interest-based financing — at the system's peak, more than half of all arable land in the Empire was classified as waqf. Islamic finance's prohibition of riba (interest/usury) is rooted in similar principles: money is a medium of exchange, not a commodity that should grow by itself, and the lender benefits without sharing risk.

Heterodox economist **Steve Keen** demonstrates empirically that the **acceleration** of mortgage debt — not its level — drives house prices. Before financial deregulation in the early 1980s, total US private debt was 61% of GDP; by 2007, it had reached 197%. Keen proposes limiting mortgages to no more than 10× annual rental income of the property (PILL — Property Income Limited Leverage) to break the amplifying feedback loop between leverage and prices.

**Honest caveat:** Mainstream economists generally view mortgage lending as beneficial — enabling homeownership that otherwise wouldn't occur, building equity through leveraged appreciation, and benefiting borrowers during inflationary periods when they repay in cheaper dollars. The heterodox critique from Hudson and Keen represents a minority academic position, though it gained considerable credibility after the 2008 crisis validated many of their predictions.

### 2.4 Exclusionary zoning is the single largest supply constraint

**Finding:** In the median US city, approximately **75% of residential land** is zoned exclusively for single-family homes — effectively banning apartments, duplexes, and other affordable housing types on most buildable land. The United States is estimated to be **3.5–4 million housing units short** of demand, with some estimates as high as 7.3 million for affordable rentals. NBER researchers consistently find that regulation — not construction costs, labor, or land — is the "single most important influence on the supply of homes." Tokyo, which uses only 12 national zoning categories and permits housing by right in nearly all zones, builds 130,000–150,000 units annually and maintains stable prices despite being one of the world's densest metro areas.

**Single-family zoning by city:**

| City | % Residential Land Single-Family Only |
|------|---------------------------------------|
| San Jose | **94%** |
| Arlington, TX | **89%** |
| Charlotte | **84%** |
| Seattle | **81%** |
| Los Angeles | **72–80%** |
| Minneapolis (pre-reform) | **70%** |
| San Francisco | **38%** (85% in Bay Area) |
| New York City | **15%** |

**Minneapolis 2040 reform — the strongest evidence that zoning reform works:** Minneapolis eliminated single-family zoning citywide in 2020, allowing duplexes and triplexes on all residential lots and removing parking minimums. The results after five years are striking: housing stock grew **12%** (vs. 4% in the rest of Minnesota), while rents grew just **1%** (vs. 14% statewide). A 2025 academic study using synthetic controls found the reform reduced home prices **16–34%** and rents **17–34%** below what they would have been without the change.

**California's ADU revolution** demonstrates that incremental reform can also produce meaningful results. After major ADU (accessory dwelling unit) reforms beginning in 2016, annual permitting increased by **15,334%** — reaching 83,865 ADUs permitted through 2022. By 2022, ADUs constituted **19% of all new housing units produced** in California.

**The Tokyo counterexample** is the strongest international evidence. Japan's national zoning system has only 12 categories (vs. hundreds across US cities), zones are additive (housing is permitted in nearly all non-industrial zones), and building is by right — no public hearings, no neighborhood veto, no design review. If a building meets parametric rules for height, setbacks, and floor-area ratio, it gets built. Tokyo produces 130,000–150,000 new units annually, maintaining slack in the market. Since 2000, Tokyo housing prices have been roughly constant while NYC and SF prices more than doubled. Japanese homes also depreciate rapidly (wooden homes over ~22 years for tax purposes), meaning housing functions as a consumable good rather than a speculative asset — eliminating NIMBY incentives.

**NIMBYism operates through rational but collectively harmful self-interest.** Homeowner wealth depends on housing scarcity — their home is typically their largest asset. William Fischel's "homevoter hypothesis" demonstrates that homeowners act as rational agents to protect property values through restrictive zoning. Research shows those who attend local planning meetings are disproportionately older, retired, white homeowners opposed to development, while renters and young people are systematically underrepresented.

**Housing deficit estimates converge around 3.5–4 million units:**

| Source | Estimate | Year |
|--------|----------|------|
| NAHB | 1.5 million | Latest |
| Freddie Mac | 3.7 million | Q3 2024 |
| Up for Growth | 3.8 million | 2025 |
| NAR | 5.5 million | 2021 |
| NLIHC (rental only) | 7.3 million | 2024 |

### 2.5 US construction trades durability for speed

**Finding:** American residential construction is overwhelmingly light wood-frame with typical effective lifespans of **50–70 years**, compared to **80–120+ years** for European brick and masonry construction. German Passivhaus standards achieve **75–90% energy savings** at just 5–10% cost premium. Japan's earthquake-resistant building standards, continuously revised after every major seismic event, represent the global gold standard for structural safety. A fundamental incentive misalignment drives US quality issues: developers profit from speed and volume of sales, not building longevity.

| Country | Typical Residential Lifespan | Dominant Construction Type |
|---------|------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Japan | 22–30 years (wooden) | Wood-frame; frequent replacement |
| United States | 50–70 years | Light wood-frame |
| United Kingdom | 80–100+ years | Brick/masonry |
| Germany | 80–120+ years | Masonry, concrete, heavy insulation |

The Passivhaus standard limits space heating demand to **≤15 kWh/m²/year** versus 75–150 kWh/m²/year for typical US construction, achieved through superinsulation, airtightness (≤0.6 ACH vs. 3–5 ACH for US code), and balanced mechanical ventilation with heat recovery. Such homes can be heated by the equivalent of a hairdryer. Exeter City Council in the UK initially faced a 20% premium for Passivhaus social housing but now achieves **4% cost savings** versus conventional construction as supply chains mature.

## SECTION 3: THE HUMAN COST

### 3.1 The homeownership wealth gap has become a chasm

**Finding:** Homeowners have a median net worth of **$396,200** compared to just **$10,400** for renters — a **38-to-1 ratio** that has widened dramatically. This gap increased by 70% over the 33 years from 1989 to 2022. Housing equity accounts for roughly **half of the total wealth gap** between white and minority households. The Black-White homeownership gap of **28 percentage points** (45.9% vs. 73.8%) has barely budged since the Fair Housing Act of 1968 and is directly traceable to federal policies of redlining and discriminatory FHA lending.

For families in the middle of the wealth distribution, **housing equity represents approximately half of total wealth** — making homeownership the primary vehicle for middle-class wealth building. The Federal Reserve's 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances found homeowners' median net worth grew to $396,200, while renters' remained at $10,400. The Urban Institute found the *average* wealth gap exceeded **$1.37 million** — a historic high.

This gap compounds generationally. Parental homeownership increases a young adult's probability of becoming a homeowner by **7–8 percentage points**. Among white parents, 72% remained homeowners from 1999 to 2015, compared with only 31% of Black parents. In Canada, children of homeowners are twice as likely to own a home.

The racial homeownership gap is a direct product of federal policy. Richard Rothstein documents in *The Color of Law* that the FHA's **1938 Underwriting Manual** recommended racially restrictive covenants and directed lending away from Black neighborhoods. NCRC research shows homeownership rates in formerly "Best"-rated HOLC neighborhoods still exceed 80%, while "Hazardous" (redlined) neighborhoods remain below 50% — a gap persisting 80+ years later. The Black-White homeownership gap was **the same in 2020 as it was in 1970** (US Treasury). Home equity accounts for close to **half of the median wealth gap** between white and minority households (Federal Reserve FEDS paper, 2023). The Great Recession was racially asymmetric: housing equity fell 41% for White households but 53% for Black and over 70% for Hispanic households.

---

### 3.2 A generational betrayal backed by polling data

**Finding:** Baby Boomers purchased homes at **2–3× income ratios**, watched them appreciate dramatically, and now constitute the demographic backbone of NIMBY opposition to new housing. Millennials — the highest-educated generation in history — are the most delayed in homeownership. Among Gen Z, **62% worry they will never own a home**, and 46% agree with the statement "no matter how hard I work, I will never be able to afford a home I really love." Researchers describe a growing phenomenon of **"financial nihilism"** among young people who have effectively abandoned the housing wealth ladder.

Half of Boomers bought their first home for **$75,000 or less**; 64% paid under $100,000. Home prices have grown **408% since 1985** while incomes grew only 241%. If home prices had merely tracked inflation since 1970, the median home would cost $177,788 today instead of $408,100. Yet **54% of Boomer homeowners never plan to sell** their homes, and only 6% say their biggest challenge when buying was that homes were too expensive — while **42% believe younger generations had it easier.**

The pattern repeats internationally. In the UK, 50% of first-time buyers received parental financial help, and half of those said they couldn't have bought without it. In Australia, homeownership among those born in the 1980s declined to 45%, compared to 65% for those born in the 1950s. In Canada, the price-to-income ratio has risen roughly 37% above 2015 levels. Across all these countries, the political dynamic is the same: those who benefited from affordable housing now oppose new construction that would extend the same opportunity to the next generation.

## SECTION 4: WHAT WORKS — ALTERNATIVE MODELS

### 4.1 Islamic finance offers a structurally different approach, with honest limitations

Islamic mortgages avoid interest (riba) by structuring transactions around real asset ownership, cost-plus sales, or partnerships rather than money-lending. Three main models are used in practice:

**Murabaha (cost-plus financing)** works like this: instead of lending you money to buy a house, the bank buys the house itself, then immediately sells it to you at a higher, pre-agreed price. You know the full cost upfront — say the bank buys for $300,000 and sells to you for $380,000. You pay that $380,000 in installments over 25 years. The bank's profit comes from the markup on a real transaction, not from interest on a loan. The total price is fixed and cannot change.

**Musharaka Mutanaqisa (diminishing partnership)** is the model preferred by Islamic scholars and dominant in the US and UK markets. You and the bank buy the house together as co-owners. If you put 20% down, you own 20% and the bank owns 80%. Each month, part of your payment "buys out" the bank's share, and part covers "rent" for the portion the bank still owns. Over time, your ownership grows and the bank's shrinks until you own 100%. This involves genuine risk-sharing — if the property loses value, both parties bear the loss proportionally.

**Ijara (lease-to-own)** means the bank buys the property and leases it to you. You pay rent, and a portion goes toward eventually purchasing the home. Title doesn't transfer until repayment is complete.

**Market scale and providers:** The global Islamic finance industry has reached approximately **$5.4 trillion** in total assets. In the US, **Guidance Residential** dominates with over **$10 billion in financing provided** to more than **40,000 families** since 2002, representing roughly 80% of the US market. Guidance uses the diminishing partnership model, with both buyer and Guidance co-owning the property through an LLC. Their contracts are backed by Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae. In the UK, **Al Rayan Bank** (£2.84 billion in assets) and **Gatehouse Bank** are the primary providers, with approximately 3,000 Islamic mortgages issued annually across about 20 institutions.

**Performance during 2008:** An IMF Working Paper analyzing ~120 banks across 8 countries found Islamic banks' profitability was better contained in 2008 because they had no exposure to mortgage-backed securities, derivatives, or speculative products prohibited by Sharia law. They came through the initial crisis "largely unscathed," though they were not immune to the subsequent real-economy downturn.

**The honest critique:** This is a vigorous debate both within the Muslim community and in academia. Critics note that Islamic mortgages routinely use LIBOR/SOFR as benchmarks for calculating rent/profit rates, the total amount paid is often comparable to a conventional mortgage, and banks structure products to minimize their risk in ways that mimic conventional lending. A ResearchGate paper describes "the Convergence Paradox" — Islamic finance has expanded into a trillion-dollar industry while its products have become "outwardly identical" to conventional finance. However, genuine structural differences do exist: co-ownership, shared risk in property devaluation, no compounding interest, and no pursuit of the homeowner's other assets in foreclosure. The most balanced assessment is that current Islamic mortgages are **legally and structurally distinct with meaningful consumer protections, but economically similar in outcome.**

### 4.2 Community Land Trusts keep housing affordable permanently

A Community Land Trust is a nonprofit that buys land and holds it permanently "in trust" for the community. When someone buys a home on CLT land, they purchase **only the building** — not the land underneath, which they lease for 99 years (renewable and inheritable). The critical mechanism: when the homeowner sells, a **resale formula** lets them earn some equity (typically 25% of appreciation) but keeps the price affordable for the next buyer. Unlike traditional affordable housing subsidies that "time out" after 15–30 years, **CLT affordability is perpetual** — the initial subsidy gets recycled indefinitely through every subsequent sale.

**The Champlain Housing Trust** in Burlington, Vermont — the largest CLT in the United States — was founded in 1984 under Mayor Bernie Sanders. It now holds **2,650+ homes** (about 18% of Burlington's residential units) and serves three northwest Vermont counties. Homes are affordable to households earning roughly **57% of area median income**. During the Great Recession, **not a single CHT family lost their home** while the broader market collapsed. Remarkably, **74% of CLT homeowners who moved on went on to purchase market-rate homes** — demonstrating the model works as a wealth-building stepping stone, not just a subsidy.

The **2022 Census of Community Land Trusts** identified **314 CLT and shared equity organizations** operating in 46 US states, holding over **40,000 housing units.** In England, 350 CLTs have completed 1,711 affordable homes with 5,413 in the pipeline.

**Scalability challenges are real.** CLTs must acquire land — often requiring grants or donated public land. Initial development depends on government subsidies, though the subsidy is "recycled" through perpetual affordability. Most CLTs remain small (a few dozen to a few hundred units). Lenders have historically been reluctant to finance homes on leased land, though Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac now recognize CLT ground leases. The model works well where it exists but has struggled to scale beyond niche applications.

### 4.3 Rent-to-own promises have mostly collapsed, but renter equity is emerging

Rent-to-own startups attracted billions in venture capital on the promise of helping renters become homeowners. The most prominent, **Divvy Homes**, raised over $400 million, was valued at nearly $2 billion, and accumulated roughly 7,000 homes. The model: Divvy buys a home the customer selects, the customer pays monthly with ~75% going to rent and ~25% to "home savings," and after three years can buy at a predetermined price. Divvy's CEO claimed roughly 50% of customers converted to ownership.

**The model collapsed when interest rates spiked.** By late 2023, Divvy had conducted three rounds of layoffs and stopped acquiring homes. In 2024–25, it was acquired by Brookfield Properties in what industry insiders called "a bloodbath for investors and employee equity." The fundamental problem: homes purchased three to four years prior at low rates became unaffordable for occupants to buy at current rates. Customer complaints accumulated — unreturned equity savings, maintenance neglect, opaque fees.

**More promising are emerging "renter equity" models.** The Enterprise Community Partners Renter Wealth Creation Fund has raised **$112 million** from impact investors to acquire affordable housing properties that reward tenants with **monthly cash back and a share of property appreciation.** Colorado's Proposition 123 set aside 0.1% of state income tax revenue (~$300 million annually) for affordable housing, including a Renter Rewards program. The concept was pioneered by Cincinnati's Cornerstone program (2000–2012), where 24 low-income households accumulated over $140,000 in equity credits. These models remain experimental and small-scale, but represent the most promising frontier for addressing the renter wealth gap.

### 4.4 Four global models that actually work — and what they teach

**Vienna: 100 years of political commitment to social housing.** Approximately **60% of Vienna's residents** live in subsidized housing. The city directly owns ~220,000 municipal apartments (~25% of stock), with another ~150,000 managed by limited-profit housing associations. The roots trace to "Red Vienna" in the 1920s, when the Social Democratic government built over 60,000 apartments in a decade — entire "cities within cities" with courtyards, kindergartens, libraries, and communal amenities. The iconic Karl-Marx-Hof (1927–30) remains one of the world's longest residential buildings. Today, income eligibility thresholds are deliberately high — roughly 75–80% of Vienna's population qualifies, creating genuinely **mixed-income communities** where doctors live alongside bus drivers. Modern developments undergo architectural design competitions and feature green energy systems and roof gardens. The system exerts a price-dampening effect on the entire private market — landlords must compete with affordable public alternatives. **Honest critique:** A 2023 AEI report found roughly one-third of older municipal units lack modern amenities. Income is checked only at application — once housed, tenants stay regardless of income increases, creating insider-outsider dynamics. The model requires sustained ~€600 million in annual subsidy and benefited from unique circumstances (decades of population decline enabling cheap land acquisition).

**Singapore: 80% public housing with homeownership built in.** The Housing Development Board (HDB) houses roughly **80% of Singapore's population**, with about 90% of residents *owning* their flats on 99-year leases. The government builds, citizens buy at heavily subsidized prices using mandatory CPF (Central Provident Fund) savings — **8 in 10 first-timer families** can cover their entire mortgage payment through CPF with zero cash outlay. New towns are planned with extensive amenities — up to half of land reserved for parks, schools, and commercial areas. Different flat types are spread across all districts to prevent income segregation. An Ethnic Integration Policy (1989) imposes racial quotas at the block level to maintain diversity. **Key concern:** As 99-year leases run down, older flats face significant value decline, creating anxiety for owners of aging properties.

**Finland: Housing First works and saves money.** As detailed above, Finland's approach of providing permanent housing first — with no preconditions — has reduced long-term homelessness by roughly 72% since 2008 while saving €15,000–52,000 per person per year. The policy required converting shelters to apartments, sustained cross-party political support over 15+ years, and a strong nonprofit sector (the Y-Foundation is Finland's fourth-largest landlord). The model's vulnerability to austerity politics was demonstrated in 2024–25 when government budget cuts caused the first uptick in homelessness in over a decade.

**Tokyo: Abundant building keeps prices stable.** Tokyo builds 130,000–150,000 new housing units annually through a national zoning system with just 12 categories, by-right construction without public hearings, and housing permitted in nearly all non-industrial zones. Since 2000, Tokyo's house prices have been roughly constant while NYC and SF doubled. In Minato ward, population increased 66% in two decades with minimal price impact — the same growth in San Francisco triggered a 231% price increase. Japan's building depreciation policy (wooden homes depreciate to zero over ~22 years) means housing is treated as a consumable, not a speculative asset. The trade-off: homeowners cannot build wealth through appreciation, and the constant demolition-rebuilding cycle generates significant waste.

### 4.5 Policy reforms show mixed but instructive results

**Vacancy taxes reduce empty homes but don't fix affordability alone.** Vancouver's Empty Homes Tax (introduced 2017 at 1%, now 3%) reduced vacant properties by **58–67%** and generated **$194.3 million** for affordable housing. For the first time in 2024, vacant homes fell below 1,000. However, a C.D. Howe Institute assessment found the tax did not affect average rents — suggesting complementary supply-side measures are essential.

**Rent control protects current tenants but can reduce supply.** The landmark Stanford study (Diamond, McQuade, Qian, 2019, *American Economic Review*) found San Francisco's rent control expansion increased tenants' probability of staying by ~20% and especially benefited older residents and minorities. But landlords responded by reducing rental housing supply by **15%** — converting to condos and redeveloping to exempt luxury buildings — causing a **5.1% city-wide rent increase.** The emerging consensus: rent control helps current tenants significantly but can backfire if landlords have easy exit options. Design details matter enormously.

**Foreign buyer bans have limited impact.** New Zealand banned most non-resident foreign buyers in 2018, but foreign purchases had constituted under 3% of transactions nationally. Prices continued rising for several years before declining for unrelated reasons (rate hikes, economic slowdown). Treasury assessed the ban was "unlikely to have substantial ongoing impact" since key drivers — supply constraints, interest rates, and tax settings — were unaffected.

**Institutional investor restrictions are politically popular but economically marginal.** In March 2026, the US Senate passed the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act (89–10) banning large institutional investors from purchasing single-family homes. However, the Urban Institute notes firms owning 100+ homes control only **1.4% of occupied single-family stock**, and 80% of their holdings are concentrated in just 5% of US counties. Impact on national prices would be "likely limited."

## SECTION 5: KEY ARGUMENTS — EVIDENCE AND COUNTERARGUMENTS

### "The housing shortage is largely artificial — created by policy choices, not physical constraints"

**This is the claim with the strongest evidence behind it.** NBER researchers Gyourko and Molloy find "regulation appears to be the single most important influence on the supply of homes." Glaeser, Gyourko, and Saks found Manhattan housing prices are "more than twice their supply costs" with land-use restrictions as the "natural explanation." Hsieh and Moretti (2019) calculate exclusionary zoning lowered US GDP by **8.9%** between 1964 and 2009. Tokyo's counterexample — stable prices despite 37 million metro population — demonstrates that abundant by-right building prevents the crisis entirely. **The strongest counterargument:** a 2025 NBER Working Paper finds "land availability is a key source of supply constraints in the most expensive locations," and construction costs, labor shortages, and infrastructure requirements are genuine barriers. The more precise formulation: **in most high-cost US markets, policy choices are the dominant barrier, accounting for the majority of the price premium above construction costs.**

### "Current zoning laws are a form of class exclusion"

**The historical and empirical evidence is overwhelming.** Rothstein's *The Color of Law* documents how explicitly racial zoning was replaced by economically exclusionary zoning after *Buchanan v. Warley* (1917). NBER research on Chicago's original zoning found "strong evidence of inequitable treatment" — Black neighborhoods were targeted for manufacturing zoning. A 2025 study in the *Journal of Politics* using Great Migration instrumental variables found racial diversity directly drove exclusionary zoning adoption. Present-day data confirms: neighborhoods zoned single-family are less diverse, wealthier, and have more homeowners. Simply allowing apartments on a block is associated with a 5.77% increase in the local Hispanic population. **Counterargument:** Zoning originated partly to address genuine nuisances — separating industrial pollution from homes. The Supreme Court upheld community homogeneity as a legitimate interest in *Belle Terre v. Boras* (1974). However, these legitimate purposes do not justify reserving 75% of residential land nationwide exclusively for single-family homes.

### "Institutional investors have measurably worsened housing affordability"

**Moderately supported in concentrated markets, weak nationally.** Academic research finds one standard deviation higher institutional purchases leads to **1.46 percentage points higher housing price growth** (Garriga, Gete, Tsouderou), and institutional investors raise rents through market power in concentrated areas (Coven, NYU). In Atlanta, institutional investors purchased 5.4% of all housing stock from 2012–2021. **The strongest counterargument:** institutional investors own just 3% of SFRs nationally and less than 0.5% of total single-family stock. They provided valuable liquidity during the post-2008 recovery and are shifting toward build-to-rent, which expands supply. The housing crisis is fundamentally a supply problem driven by zoning, not investor participation.

### "Housing is a human right, not a commodity"

**The principle is well-established in international law** — Article 25 of the UDHR, the ICESCR (1966), and multiple national constitutions recognize the right to adequate housing. The OHCHR establishes housing as a precondition for exercising rights to work, health, and political participation. **The strongest counterargument** is practical, not moral: simply declaring a right without implementing effective supply-side policy achieves nothing. Countries that have enshrined housing rights constitutionally (South Africa) still have massive shortages. The right framing is necessary but not sufficient — it provides moral foundation but must be coupled with the concrete reforms documented in this report.

### "The mortgage interest system transfers wealth from the working class to financial institutions"

**Both sides present compelling evidence.** The raw numbers are shocking — $418,527 in interest on a $300,000 loan at 7%, with 85% of early payments being pure interest. An estimated $500–650 billion flows annually in mortgage interest. Hudson's work documents how the FIRE sector extracts economic rent from the productive economy. **The strongest counterargument:** without mortgage lending, only the wealthy could buy homes, and homeowners still accumulate median net worth of $396,200 versus $10,400 for renters. Fixed-rate borrowers benefit from inflation (repaying in cheaper dollars) and keep 100% of appreciation. The system both enables wealth building for those who access it and extracts significant wealth from those who carry the debt.

### "Interest-free housing finance is viable at scale"

**Viable for niche markets; faces real scalability barriers.** The Islamic finance industry's $5.4 trillion in assets and Guidance Residential's 40,000+ US families demonstrate the model works. The diminishing partnership model's compatibility with Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac is promising. Crisis resilience is documented. **The strongest counterargument:** the economic equivalence critique is damaging — if the total cost is comparable and pricing benchmarks to conventional rates, the reform is structural rather than substantive. Higher costs (25–30% premium in the UK), talent shortages, and capital allocation challenges limit scale. The model serves faith-based communities effectively but is unlikely to replace conventional mortgages systemically without deeper structural changes.

### "Renters are subsidizing landlords' wealth accumulation with no equity return"

**Moderately supported with important caveats.** The 38-to-1 wealth gap between homeowners and renters is real and has widened 70% over 33 years. Rent payments explicitly build landlord equity while tenants accumulate nothing. Home equity accounts for roughly half the total wealth gap. **The strongest counterargument:** selection effects matter — homeowners are wealthier for reasons beyond housing, including higher incomes and more financial literacy. Landlords bear genuine risks (vacancy, maintenance, liability). Renting provides flexibility and eliminates property taxes, transaction costs, and maintenance obligations. The most precise framing: **the system is designed so that the primary wealth-building mechanism is inaccessible to renters, who subsidize others' wealth building through rent without any equity path** — but the fix should be creating alternative wealth-building pathways (like renter equity models), not necessarily making everyone a homeowner.

## Conclusion: the crisis is severe, but solutions exist

The data paints an unambiguous picture. Housing has become unaffordable across the developed world through a convergence of financialization, exclusionary zoning, underbuilding, and the transformation of shelter into a speculative asset. The US price-to-income ratio has more than doubled since 1970. Half of renters are cost-burdened. A minimum-wage worker cannot afford a modest apartment anywhere in the country. Homelessness hit an all-time record. The racial wealth gap — roughly half attributable to homeownership disparities — has not improved since the Fair Housing Act.

Three insights emerge from the evidence that challenge conventional framing. First, **the housing crisis is primarily a supply crisis driven by policy, not by physical or economic constraints.** Tokyo proves that a megacity can maintain stable housing prices through abundant by-right construction. Minneapolis proved that even partial zoning reform moderates rents within five years. The estimated US housing deficit of 3.5–4 million units is a direct product of reserving 75% of residential land for single-family homes only.

Second, **the financial architecture of housing systematically transfers wealth upward.** Mortgage amortization front-loads interest so that most homeowners never reach meaningful equity building before selling or refinancing. An estimated $500–650 billion flows annually in mortgage interest from households to financial institutions. This is not a conspiracy — it is a structural feature of a system designed to serve lenders.

Third, **workable alternatives are not theoretical — they are operating at scale.** Vienna has housed 60% of its population in subsidized mixed-income housing for a century. Singapore has housed 80% of its citizens in government-built flats. Finland has reduced homelessness by 72% through Housing First while saving money. Community land trusts have kept housing permanently affordable with zero foreclosures during the Great Recession. The question is not whether solutions exist, but whether there is sufficient political will to implement them against the organized interests of those who profit from the status quo.

The most actionable reforms, ranked by evidence strength: legalize multifamily housing on most residential land (strongest evidence), fund Housing First programs for homelessness (proven cost-effective), scale community land trusts through public land disposition and subsidy, reform mortgage structures to reduce front-loaded interest extraction, and implement vacancy taxes as complements to supply-side reforms. What will not work: foreign buyer bans (minimal impact), institutional investor bans (too small a share of the market), or rent control without supply expansion (reduces available units). The path forward requires treating housing as infrastructure — essential, publicly supported, and too important to leave entirely to speculative markets.
