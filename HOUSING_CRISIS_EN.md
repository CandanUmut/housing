# 🏠 The Housing Crisis: How the System Stopped Working for People

> **The world's wealthiest nations have made housing unaffordable for the majority of their working populations — and the data shows this was a policy choice, not an inevitability.**

In the United States, the median home now costs five times the median household income, up from roughly 2.3 times in 1970. Half of all American renters spend more than 30% of their income on housing. A minimum-wage worker would need to work 116 hours per week — nearly three full-time jobs — to afford a modest two-bedroom apartment anywhere in the country. Homelessness hit an all-time record of 771,480 people in 2024. These are not natural market outcomes. They reflect decades of deliberate policy decisions that transformed shelter into a speculative asset class, restricted supply through exclusionary zoning, and channeled hundreds of billions in annual mortgage interest from working families to financial institutions.

### 1.4 The True Cost of a Mortgage

The word **"mortgage"** derives from Old French *mort gage* — literally **"death pledge."** The financial reality lives up to the etymology.

**$300,000 home — 3% vs. 7% interest (30-year fixed):**

| Metric | At 3.0% (2020) | At 7.0% (2024) | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monthly payment | $1,265 | **$1,996** | +$731/mo (+58%) |
| Total paid over 30 years | $455,332 | **$718,527** | +$263,195 |
| Total interest paid | $155,332 | **$418,527** | +$263,195 |
| Interest as % of total | 34.1% | **58.2%** | — |
| Total paid as multiple of price | 1.52× | **2.40×** | — |

**At 7%, the buyer pays $418,527 in interest alone — $118,527 more than the home's purchase price.**

**In year 1 of a 30-year mortgage at current rates, 85% of each monthly payment is pure interest.** The "tipping point" — where more of each payment goes to principal than interest — doesn't arrive until approximately **year 19**. The average homeowner sells after roughly **8 years**, meaning most never reach meaningful equity building. *(Source: Bankrate, Freddie Mac)*

### 1.5 Homelessness Hit an All-Time Record

The 2024 HUD Point-in-Time count recorded **771,480 homeless people** — the highest figure ever recorded, an **18.1% increase** over 2023, and the largest single-year jump in the survey's history. Family homelessness surged **39.4%**.

| Year | Total Homeless | Context |
|---|---|---|
| 2007 | 647,258 | Baseline |
| 2010 | ~649,917 | Post-recession peak |
| 2016 | 549,928 | Historic low |
| 2023 | 653,104 | Prior record |
| **2024** | **771,480** | **All-time record (+18.1%)** |

*(Source: HUD Exchange, 2024)*

**Hidden homelessness dwarfs the official count:** approximately **3.7 million people** live in doubled-up arrangements, and **1.4 million children** were identified as homeless by schools — 84% of them doubled up or in motels, excluded from HUD's count.

**Annual eviction filings:** Princeton's Eviction Lab estimates ~**2.3 million filings** and ~**900,000 actual evictions** pre-pandemic. Phoenix alone recorded **86,946 filings in 2024** — one every six minutes.

**Finland's Housing First** demonstrates this is a policy choice, not an inevitability: by guaranteeing permanent housing with no preconditions, Finland reduced long-term homelessness by **~72%** from 2008 to 2024, saving **€15,000–52,000 per person per year** compared to managing chronic homelessness through emergency services.

## 2. How the System Was Broken — And By Whom

### 2.1 Housing Became a Financial Instrument

Housing's transformation from shelter to speculative asset occurred in three stages:

**Phase 1 — Securitization (1968–1983):** The 1968 Housing and Urban Development Act created Ginnie Mae and privatized Fannie Mae. By 1983, Freddie Mac issued the first collateralized mortgage obligation (CMO). Scholars identify the 1970s as when "the decoupling of the financial side of housing from real economic activity" began. *(Source: Socio-Economic Review, Oxford Academic)*

**Phase 2 — Deregulation and 2008 (1990s–2008):** The 1999 repeal of Glass-Steagall removed barriers between commercial and investment banking. Private-label securitization exploded, feeding the subprime crisis. In 2008, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac entered federal conservatorship — where they remain, with **$7.7 trillion** in combined outstanding securities.

**Phase 3 — QE and Financialization 2.0 (2009–present):** The Federal Reserve purchased nearly **90%** of the increase in eligible mortgages from 2020 to 2022. An IMF Working Paper (2023) confirmed that while monetary easing reduces mortgage payment burdens, it *"raises house prices, lowers housing affordability for first-time homebuyers, and increases housing wealth inequality."* *(Source: Brookings Institution, IMF Working Paper 2023)*

> As of Q4 2024, US household real estate totaled **$48.1 trillion** — approximately 28.4% of total household net worth of $169.4 trillion. Globally, residential real estate comprises **$163 trillion**, roughly 75% of all global real estate value. The UN Special Rapporteur on Housing described the shift: *"Housing is treated as a commodity — a vehicle for wealth and investment — rather than a social good."* *(Source: Federal Reserve Z.1, OHCHR)*

### 2.2 Wall Street Bought the Neighborhood — With Important Nuance

After 2008, institutional investors purchased foreclosed homes at discount prices. The six largest corporate landlords now own approximately **430,000 homes**.

| Company | Homes Owned |
|---|---|
| Progress Residential (Pretium) | ~100,000 |
| Invitation Homes | 97,036 |
| Blackstone (via HPA + Tricon) | ~62,000 |
| American Homes 4 Rent | 60,337 |
| Amherst Group | ~59,400 |
| FirstKey Homes (Cerberus) | 52,000+ |

**Documented impacts:** A Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta study found corporate owners of 15+ properties were **8% more likely** to file eviction notices than small landlords. In Atlanta specifically, the institutional investor eviction filing rate was **20%** — nearly three times the overall SFR rate of 7%. *(Source: Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta, SSRN)*

**The important caveat:** These six firms own just **~3% of single-family rentals nationally** and less than **0.5% of total single-family housing stock**. Mom-and-pop landlords (1–10 properties) own **82.6%** of all single-family rentals. The widespread claim that "BlackRock is buying all homes" is factually wrong — Blackstone (a different company) owns approximately **0.06%** of US single-family homes. Impact is real but intensely local, not national.

**The Airbnb effect:** In Barcelona, Airbnb increased rents by **1.9%** and transaction prices by **4.6–5.3%** for the average neighborhood, with larger effects in tourist-heavy areas. NYC's Comptroller estimated a **10%** rental price increase from 2009–2016 attributable to Airbnb. *(Source: Journal of Urban Economics, García-López et al., 2020)*

### 2.3 The Interest/Debt Machine

**At current rates (6–7%), 85% of the first year's mortgage payments is pure interest.** The tipping point where principal exceeds interest doesn't arrive until year 19 of a 30-year loan.

An estimated **$500–650 billion flows annually** in mortgage interest from US households to financial institutions — derived from approximately $13.2 trillion in household mortgage debt. *(Source: Federal Reserve, Michael Hudson)*

Heterodox economist **Steve Keen** demonstrates that mortgage debt acceleration — not its level — drives house prices. Before financial deregulation in the early 1980s, total US private debt was **61% of GDP**; by 2007, it had reached **197%**. He proposes limiting mortgages to no more than **10× annual rental income** of a property to break the feedback loop between leverage and prices.

**Historical context:** Every major civilization has restricted or banned usury. The medieval Catholic Church explicitly banned it, with the Third Lateran Council (1179) imposing excommunication on usurers. Thomas Aquinas argued charging interest was "selling the same thing twice." The Ottoman Empire's **waqf system** funded housing without interest-based financing — at its peak, more than **half of all arable land** in the Empire was classified as waqf. Islamic finance's prohibition of **riba** (interest) rests on the same principle: money is a medium of exchange, not a commodity that should grow by itself.

### 2.4 Zoning as Class Exclusion

In the median US city, approximately **75% of residential land is zoned exclusively for single-family homes** — effectively banning apartments, duplexes, and other affordable housing types on most buildable land.

| City | % Residential Land Single-Family Only |
|---|---|
| San Jose | 94% |
| Arlington, TX | 89% |
| Charlotte | 84% |
| Seattle | 81% |
| Los Angeles | 72–80% |
| Minneapolis (pre-reform) | 70% |
| San Francisco | 38% |
| New York City | 15% |

The United States is estimated to be **3.5–4 million housing units short** of demand, with some estimates as high as 7.3 million for affordable rentals. NBER researchers find regulation is *"the single most important influence on the supply of homes."*

**The Minneapolis proof:** In 2020, Minneapolis eliminated single-family zoning citywide. Results after five years: housing stock grew **12%** (vs. 4% in the rest of Minnesota); rents grew just **1%** (vs. 14% statewide). A 2025 academic study found the reform reduced home prices **16–34%** and rents **17–34%** below what they would have been. *(Source: The Pew Charitable Trusts, 2025)*

**The Tokyo counterexample:** Japan uses only **12 national zoning categories** (vs. hundreds in US cities), building is by right with no public hearings, and housing is permitted in nearly all non-industrial zones. Tokyo produces **130,000–150,000 units annually** and has maintained roughly stable prices since 2000 — while NYC and SF prices more than doubled. Japan's building depreciation policy (wooden homes depreciate over ~22 years) means housing functions as a consumable, not a speculative asset, **eliminating NIMBY incentives entirely.** *(Source: Southernurbanism)*

**Why NIMBYism persists:** Homeowner wealth depends on housing scarcity — their home is typically their largest asset. Research shows local planning meetings are dominated by older, retired, white homeowners opposed to development, while renters and young people are systematically underrepresented.

### 2.5 Built to Fail

| Country | Typical Residential Lifespan | Dominant Construction Type |
|---|---|---|
| United States | 50–70 years | Light wood-frame |
| Japan | 22–30 years (wooden) | Wood-frame; frequent replacement |
| United Kingdom | 80–100+ years | Brick/masonry |
| Germany | 80–120+ years | Masonry, concrete, heavy insulation |

German **Passivhaus** standard: limits space heating demand to ≤15 kWh/m²/year — versus **75–150 kWh/m²** for typical US construction. Achieved through superinsulation, airtightness, and heat recovery ventilation. Exeter City Council initially faced a 20% premium for Passivhaus social housing but now achieves **4% cost savings** versus conventional construction as supply chains mature.

**The fundamental misalignment:** Developers profit from the speed and volume of sales, not from building longevity. Buyers pay maximum price for minimum lifespan. The incentive to build well simply does not exist in the current system.

|---|---|---|---|
| Median Net Worth (2022) | **$396,200** | **$10,400** | **38-to-1** |

## 4. What Actually Works — Alternative Models

### 4.1 Interest-Free Finance — Islamic Finance Models

Islamic mortgages avoid interest (riba) by structuring transactions around real asset ownership, cost-plus sales, or partnerships. Three models are used in practice:

**Murabaha (Cost-Plus Financing)**
Instead of lending money, the institution buys the property itself, then sells it to the buyer at a disclosed, pre-agreed markup. The total price is fixed and cannot change — no compounding interest. *(Used in: US, UK, Malaysia, Gulf states)*

**Musharaka Mutanaqisa (Diminishing Partnership)** ← *preferred by Islamic scholars*
The institution and buyer co-own the property. The buyer gradually purchases the institution's share. Meanwhile, the buyer pays rent only on the portion the institution still owns. As ownership shifts, rent decreases. **Genuine risk-sharing: if property loses value, both parties bear the loss proportionally.** This is the dominant Islamic mortgage structure in the US and UK. *(Guidance Residential in US; Al Rayan Bank, Gatehouse Bank in UK)*

**Ijara (Lease-to-Own)**
The institution owns and leases the property to the buyer. Lease payments build toward ownership. Title transfers at end of term.

**Market scale:**
- Global Islamic finance industry: **$5.4 trillion** in total assets
- US market leader: **Guidance Residential** — $10+ billion in financing to 40,000+ families, backed by Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae
- UK: ~3,000 Islamic mortgages issued annually across ~20 institutions
- During 2008: Islamic banks emerged "largely unscathed" with no exposure to mortgage-backed securities *(Source: IMF Working Paper)*

**Honest assessment:** Critics note that Islamic mortgages often use SOFR as a pricing benchmark, and total amounts paid are often comparable to conventional mortgages. A genuine "Convergence Paradox" exists — Islamic finance has grown into a trillion-dollar industry while some products have become "outwardly identical" to conventional finance. However, genuine structural differences remain: co-ownership, shared risk in property devaluation, no compounding, and no pursuit of other assets in foreclosure. Current Islamic mortgages are **legally and structurally distinct**, even if economically similar in outcome.

### 4.2 Community Land Trusts — Permanently Affordable Housing

A **Community Land Trust (CLT)** is a nonprofit that permanently holds land "in trust." Residents purchase only the building — not the land — on a 99-year renewable lease. When they sell, a resale formula lets them keep some equity (~25% of appreciation) while keeping the home affordable for the next buyer. **Unlike traditional subsidies that expire after 15–30 years, CLT affordability is perpetual.**

**Champlain Housing Trust (Burlington, VT)** — oldest US CLT, founded 1984:
- 2,650+ homes (~18% of Burlington's residential units)
- Homes affordable to households earning ~57% of area median income
- **Zero foreclosures during the Great Recession** while the broader market collapsed
- **74% of CLT homeowners who moved on purchased market-rate homes** — the model works as a wealth-building stepping stone *(Source: Champlain Housing Trust)*

**Scale:** 314 CLT organizations operating in 46 US states, holding **40,000+ housing units**. In England, 350 CLTs have completed 1,711 affordable homes.

**Limitations:** Requires public land or grants for land acquisition. Lenders have historically been reluctant to finance homes on leased land (though Fannie/Freddie now recognize CLT ground leases). Requires ongoing nonprofit management. Works well where it exists but has struggled to scale without public investment.

### 4.3 Renter Equity — Building Wealth Without Owning

The core injustice: **renters pay monthly for decades and own nothing at the end.**

Emerging models are beginning to address this:

- **Enterprise Community Partners Renter Wealth Creation Fund:** $112 million raised from impact investors; rewards tenants with monthly cash back and a share of property appreciation
- **Colorado Proposition 123:** 0.1% of state income tax (~$300 million/year) directed to affordable housing, including a Renter Rewards program
- **Cornerstone Program (Cincinnati, 2000–2012):** 24 low-income households accumulated over $140,000 in equity credits

**Divvy Homes warning:** The rent-to-own startup raised $400 million and was valued at $2 billion, accumulating 7,000 homes. When rates spiked, the model collapsed — acquired by Brookfield in what insiders called "a bloodbath." The lesson: private equity rent-to-own is high-risk for tenants. **Public or nonprofit versions of renter equity are more reliable.**

**Policy proposal:** Mandatory equity credit — a percentage of every rent payment credited toward future purchase of the property, guaranteed by law.

### 4.4 Four Global Models That Actually Work

**🏙️ Vienna — 100 Years of Political Commitment**
- **60% of Vienna's residents** live in subsidized housing
- ~220,000 municipal apartments (25% of stock) + 150,000 managed by limited-profit associations
- Mixed-income communities: income eligibility thresholds cover ~75–80% of Vienna's population — **doctors live alongside bus drivers**
- History: "Red Vienna" in the 1920s built 60,000 apartments in a decade, complete with kindergartens, libraries, and communal spaces
- **Exerts a price-dampening effect on the entire private market** — landlords must compete with affordable public alternatives
- Annual subsidy: ~€600 million *(Source: City of Vienna)*

**🇸🇬 Singapore — Public Housing With Ownership Built In**
- **80% of residents** in Housing Development Board (HDB) flats
- **90% of HDB residents own** their flats (on 99-year leases)
- 8 in 10 first-timer families cover entire mortgage payment through CPF savings with **zero cash outlay**
- Different flat types distributed across all districts — prevents income segregation
- Trade-off: as 99-year leases run down, older flats face significant value decline *(Source: Singapore HDB)*

**🇫🇮 Finland — Housing First**
- Provides permanent housing **immediately with no preconditions** (no required sobriety, employment, or treatment)
- Converted shelters into permanent apartments — from 2,121 shelter beds in 1985 to **52 by 2016**
- Reduced long-term homelessness **~72%** from 2008 to 2024
- Saves **€15,000–52,000 per person per year** vs. managing homelessness through emergency services
- Vulnerability: government budget cuts in 2024–25 caused the first uptick in over a decade *(Source: Housing First Europe, ARA)*

**🗼 Tokyo — Abundant Building Keeps Prices Stable**
- **12 national zoning categories** (vs. hundreds in US cities)
- Building permitted **by right** — no public hearings, no neighborhood veto
- **130,000–150,000 new units annually**
- Since 2000, Tokyo prices roughly constant while NYC and SF more than doubled
- Japan's building depreciation policy eliminates NIMBY incentives — homeowners can't speculate on scarcity
- Trade-off: constant demolition-rebuilding generates significant waste *(Source: Southernurbanism, REthink Tokyo)*

## 7. What Needs to Change

### Financial System
- Reform mortgage structures to reduce front-loaded interest extraction
- Create public banking options for mortgages — reduce private bank extraction
- Support and scale Islamic finance / interest-free alternatives as a legitimate market option
- Tax vacant investment properties *(Vancouver model: 3% vacancy tax reduced vacant homes 58–67%)*
- Regulate institutional single-family home purchases in high-concentration markets

### Supply and Zoning
- **Eliminate exclusionary single-family zoning in urban areas** *(strongest evidence)*
- Fast-track permitting for affordable and social housing
- Mandate minimum density near transit
- Support ADU construction *(California: 15,334% increase in permitting post-reform)*

### Renter Rights
- **Equity credit system:** portion of rent credited toward future purchase
- Right of first refusal: tenants offered first purchase option when landlord sells
- Rent stabilization *(not blanket rent control)* in tight markets
- Stronger anti-eviction protections

### Housing Quality
- Mandatory minimum construction lifespan standards
- Independent quality inspection at completion
- Builder liability for structural failures for minimum 20 years
- Energy efficiency standards *(Passivhaus: 75–90% energy savings at 5–10% cost premium)*

### Public and Social Housing
- **Large-scale public/social housing investment** — the US last did this seriously in the 1950s
- Community Land Trust funding and public land disposition
- **Housing First** for homelessness — proven model, expand nationally *(Finland: 72% reduction)*
- Mixed-income social housing *(Vienna model)*
